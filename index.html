Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Does the ownership of the buildings affect their affordability?
  2. Does the flip term/non-flip status of a property affect its value?
  3. Do larger flip terms allow for properties to gain value?
  4. Is the flipped-property market activly hurting the affordability of housing?

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Overall I made the following observations:

  1. The overwhelming majority of properties were purchased by non-investors, indicating a significant presence of private individuals or families in the housing market. Institutional buyers constituted a minor fraction of purchases. However these larger investors dominate the market financially, purchasing and selling much more expensive properties than the non-investors.
  2. Property prices have exhibited a clear upward trend over time, with median prices rising consistently. However, there is a divergence in median prices among different investor types, with non-investors initially having similar prices in the early 2000s but eventually being outpaced by other investor categories.
  3. The distribution of property types purchased across investor categories remains relatively proportional, indicating not only diverse investment portfolios among buyers but also no particular lean towards one typology by each investment type
  4. There is a significant correlation between seller and buyer types, with properties predominantly bought by non-investors being sold by non-investors as well. However, properties sold by non-investors are also frequently purchased by small investors.
  5. The analysis of property flips reveals that a considerable portion of flipped properties were initially sold by non-investors
  6. Geographically, non-investor sales are concentrated within the city itself, particularly in the downtown area, while other investor types exhibit different sales patterns.
  7. The downtown area emerges as a hotspot for property flips, with higher average prices for flipped properties compared to non-flipped ones. However, flipped properties do not always command higher prices, indicating nuanced market dynamics.
  8. Property flipping is prevalent in older buildings, particularly those built in the late 1800s and early 1900s, but there is also a surprising peak in flipping activities involving properties built in the early 2000s.



I was also able to answer some questions:

Does the ownership of the buildings affect their affordability?

The ownership of buildings does not directly determine affordability. However, the analysis reveals that investors tend towards purchasing from their own investment category, so a non-investor would find it challenging to afford an institutionally sold property but less so for a property sold by another non-investor.


Does the flip term/non-flip status of a property affect its value?

The flip term/non-flip status of a property does affect its value, but the relationship is nuanced. While the downtown area emerges as a hotspot for property flips, with higher average prices for flipped properties compared to non-flipped ones, flipped properties do not always command higher prices. This indicates that while property flipping may contribute to value appreciation in certain cases, other factors such as property condition, location, and market dynamics also play crucial roles in determining property value.


Do larger flip terms allow for properties to gain value?

The longer term flips are more frequent, especially in the older buildings and they were able to increase their prices more than the short- and medium-term flips. However, they do not gain value more rapidly than the non-flipped properties.


Is the flipped-property market actively hurting the affordability of housing?

Flipped properties do not always command higher prices. This suggests that while property flipping may contribute to value appreciation in certain cases, its overall impact on housing affordability is nuanced and may depend on various factors such as market dynamics, property conditions, and location. Further research specifically focused on the relationship between property flipping and housing affordability would be necessary to draw definitive conclusions.
